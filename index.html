Untangling the Relationship between Corporate Political Ties and Low-Carbon Innovation: The Moderating Roles of Prominence and Favorability
IEEE Transaction on Engineering Management, 2023
 Li Xia (First author), Qianwen Shao, Jiuchang Wei, Zhi Li
Paper Online Appendix Material (Part I)Online Appendix Material (Part II)
Abstract: The current literature has yet to reach a full consensus on the influence of corporate political ties (PT) on firm-level low-carbon innovation (LCI). At one extreme, some literature argues that PT can improve access to government-controlled material innovation resources and provide firms with privileged information on existing or forthcoming innovation policies, thus facilitating LCI. At the other end of the spectrum, others suggest that businesses with PT may be vulnerable to rent-seeking activities and suffer reduced autonomy in innovation decision-making, which appears detrimental to LCI. To reconcile the debate, this study fused and extended insights drawing upon the combination of resource dependence, rent-seeking and legitimacy theories and reasoned that both propositions may be correct over some range. As expected, we found that PT will exert a U-shaped effect on LCI. Also, it revealed that two facets of corporate reputation, namely, corporate prominence (i.e. being known) and generalized favorability (i.e. being liked), can enhance the curvilinear relationship. By collecting and analyzing a longitudinal panel data set of 3425 firms from 2009 to 2018 drawn from China – the largest emerging market economy and the biggest carbon dioxide emitter in the world, strong evidence was found for our theoretical arguments and empirical hypotheses.
Board Interlock Networks and Corporate Low-Carbon Innovation in China: Does Position Matter?
IEEE Transaction on Engineering Management, 2023
 Li Xia (First author), Shuo Gao, Qianwen Shao, Jiuchang Wei, Zhi Li
PaperOnline Appendix Material
Abstract: Drawing upon the resource dependence and social network theories, this study sheds light on how two major kinds of interlocking network positions - central network position and structural holes position - drive corporate low-carbon innovation. We consider the mediating effect of information asymmetry and knowledge absorptive capacity to reap benefits from occupying the two advantageous network positions. Our main results show that either holding a central network position or spanning a structural holes position plays a significant role in stimulating low-carbon technologies, and the promotion effect can be partially channeled through downward information asymmetry and upward knowledge absorptive capacity.
Doing well by doing good: Unpacking the black box of corporate social responsibility
Asia Pacific Journal of Management, 2023
 Li Xia (First author), Zhi Li, Jiuchang Wei, Shuo Gao
Paper
Abstract: Using the data of the listed firms in China’s renewable energy from 2010 to 2016, this study shows that overall CSR can enhance CFP. Moreover, the result also shows that, for China’s renewable energy industry, shareholder and environmental dimensions positively affect CFP while non-shareholder as well as customers, suppliers and consumers responsibility demonstrates a negative effect. No significant effect is identified between the responsibility for social contributions and CFP, and neither is between employee responsibility and CFP.
Government subsidy and corporate green innovation - does board governance play a role?

Abstract: This study explores a U-shaped relationship between government subsidies and corporate green innovation in China's new energy vehicle (NEV) industry and investigates the moderating effect of board governance within this relationship.
